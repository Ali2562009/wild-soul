I Was Lost for Years.
The Forest Brought Me Back.

I was not a bad person who did bad things. I was a good person who forgot who he was. This is how nature, silence, and God found me again — and what happened when they did.

12 min read
A true story
For anyone who has ever been lost

There was a period of my life — a few years, maybe more, I stopped counting — where I lived like God didn't exist. Not because I didn't believe. I believed. I always believed. But believing and living are two different things, and for a long time, I only did one of them.

I did what the people around me did. I went where they went. I laughed at things I shouldn't have laughed at. I stayed silent when I should have spoken. I filled my time with noise — music too loud, nights too long, days too empty — and I told myself this was living. This was freedom. This was being young.

Nobody forced me. That's the part that's hard to say out loud. Nobody held a gun to my head. I made every choice myself, with full knowledge of what I was doing, and I made peace with it the way you make peace with a slow leak — you know it's there, you just don't want to deal with it today.

· · ·

The thing about getting lost

The thing about getting lost — really lost, not just geographically but inside yourself — is that it doesn't happen suddenly. Nobody wakes up one morning and decides: today I will become someone I don't recognize. It's slower than that. It's a thousand small compromises. A thousand small yeses to the wrong things and noes to the right ones.

And the worst part? You still feel normal. You still eat breakfast. You still call your mother. You still say alhamdulillah out of habit when someone asks how you are. But somewhere underneath all of that, something in you knows. Something in you keeps a quiet, honest record of everything.

"You can fool everyone around you. You cannot fool the part of you that was made by God."

I ignored that part for years. I drowned it in distraction. I became very good at being busy, at filling every silence before it had a chance to say something I didn't want to hear.

· · ·

The day everything cracked open

I don't remember exactly when it happened. I know it was early morning. I know I hadn't slept well. I know I had driven, without really planning to, to a place outside the city where there were trees and a narrow path and nothing else.

I sat down on a rock near a small stream. I didn't plan to sit. My legs just stopped. And I remember the silence — not the absence of sound, because there were birds and water and wind — but the silence of everything I had been running from. It was all there. Waiting patiently. The way God waits.

I started crying. Not gently, not beautifully the way it happens in films. Ugly crying. The kind where your whole body is involved. Years of it, all at once, like a dam that had been holding something back for too long finally just — lets go.

أَلَا بِذِكْرِ اللَّهِ تَطْمَئِنُّ الْقُلُوبُ
"Verily, in the remembrance of God do hearts find rest." — Quran 13:28

I had memorized that verse as a child. My grandmother used to say it. I had not thought about it in years. Sitting next to that stream, with my face wet and my chest open and the trees completely indifferent to my pain, it came back to me. Not as a memory. As a fact. As something true happening inside my body right then.

Hearts find rest. Not should find rest. Not can find rest. Find rest. Present tense. Certain tense.

· · ·

What the forest understood that I didn't

I started going back. Every few days, then every day. I didn't go to pray, at first. I just went to sit. To be in a place that didn't need anything from me. That had no opinion about my past. That didn't know what I had done or not done. The trees didn't know my sins. The water didn't care about my reputation.

There is something deeply merciful about nature. It receives you exactly as you are. This, I think, is one of the ways God speaks — through things that have no voice but make you feel heard.

Slowly, something began to shift. I started to pray again — first just Fajr, because dawn in the open air feels like the closest thing to a conversation I've ever had. Then the rest. Not perfectly. Not consistently. But honestly. That was the difference this time — I was doing it because I meant it, not because I was supposed to.

I stopped some things. Not all at once — I'm not that disciplined, and I don't believe in lying to sound more impressive than I am. Slowly, like someone learning to breathe differently, I let go of the things that had been filling the space where peace was supposed to live.

"Tawbah doesn't feel like a door slamming shut on your past. It feels like a window opening. Light coming in. Air you forgot existed."

I had always imagined returning to God as something heavy — a long list of everything wrong, a courtroom, a sentence. What I found instead was almost embarrassingly simple. A stream. A rock. A verse I learned as a child. A cry I had been holding for years. And then — slowly, not all at once — something that felt like being known. And forgiven. And welcomed back.

· · ·

What I want you to know, if you are where I was

I am writing this for the person who believes but doesn't live it yet. The one who says inshallah and means it somewhere deep down, even if the rest of their life doesn't show it. The one who feels that quiet weight that I felt — that honest record being kept somewhere inside them.

You don't have to fix everything at once. You don't have to be perfect before you return. You don't have to deserve it first. That's the whole point — you don't earn your way back. You just turn. In Arabic the word is tawbah — it literally means to turn. Not to run, not to earn, not to explain. Just to turn.

Go somewhere quiet if you can. Not a mosque, necessarily — though mosques are beautiful. But anywhere without noise. A garden. A field. A park bench at five in the morning. Somewhere the world slows down enough that you can hear yourself think.

Sit down. Let the silence say what it needs to say. Let yourself feel what you've been avoiding. And then — when you're ready — just start. One prayer. One honest moment. One small turn toward the direction you always knew, in some part of yourself, that you were supposed to be facing.

قُلْ يَا عِبَادِيَ الَّذِينَ أَسْرَفُوا عَلَىٰ أَنفُسِهِمْ لَا تَقْنَطُوا مِن رَّحْمَةِ اللَّهِ
"Say: O my servants who have wronged themselves — do not despair of the mercy of God." — Quran 39:53

Do not despair. Those three words have carried more lost people home than any lecture, any guilt, any shame ever could.

I know this because they carried me.

Written somewhere outside, with dirty hands and a clean heart.
